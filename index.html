Invasive Botany of

Seacliff

Below is a list of common non-native plants found along Seacliff, as well as many other parts of coastal California. Invasive plants, in certain localities, have the potential to push out native species, negatively affecting biodiversity, which makes the local ecosystem less resilient in times of stress. They also have the potential to alter landscapes and waterways, which can wreak havoc on infrastructure, create an imbalance in ecosystems, and take up unnecessary amounts of water. This list is by no means exhaustive, but just a guide to get us thinking about the plants around us. Enjoy the scavenger hunt and plant native!

Native: A plant that has occurred naturally in an area for thousands of years and has evolved with wildlife

Non-Native/ Exotic: A plant that has been introduced to an area, but does not readily show any signs of being disruptive to a landscape

Invasive: A non-native species that causes harm to an ecosystem, essentially throwing economic, human, and environmental systems out of balance

Naturalized: Non-native species that successfully exist in an area without the assistance of humans. They do not yet show signs of being disruptive to ecosystems.

Carpobrotus edulis

Ice Plant

Status: Invasive

Country/Continent of Origin: South Africa

How it got here: Brought here for erosion control and  to stabilize soils

Effects on the ecosystem: The presence of ice plant creates thick, dense, heavy mats that cause erosion on cliff edges. It also outcompetes natives, which leads to a decline in plant and pollinator diversity

Cakile maritima

European Sea Rocket

Status: Invasive

Country/Continent of Origin: Europe, N. Africa, W. Asia

How it got here: Brought here on ship ballasts (weight placed on boats to lower the center of gravity) that contained seeds from Europe

Effects on the ecosystem: While the effects are minor, the presence of this plant outcompetes native species, as well as altering physical dune processes.

Eucalyptus globulus

Blue Gum Eucalyptus

Status: Invasive

Country/Continent of Origin: Australia

How it got here: Imported for timber production, and was being planted as windbreaks on highways and for agricultural fields

Effects on the ecosystem:  Do not host any wildlife and compete with native plants. They are also known to be very fire-prone.

Cortaderia jubata

Jubata Grass

Status: Invasive

Country/Continent of Origin: Northern Andes

How it got here: Introduced as an ornamental and as a means of erosion control

Effects on the ecosystem: This grass grows to an enormous size, making it difficult for native plants to compete and thereby reducing biodiversity. They can also alter wildlife habitats, create fire hazards, and reduce access to recreational areas

Raphanus raphanistrum

Wild radish

Status: Non-Native

Country/Continent of Origin: Europe, N. Africa, And the Middle East

How it got here: This plant is a hybrid between R. Sativus and another radish seed that was introduced accidentally through contaminated grain brought from Europe.

Effects on the ecosystem: Stands outcompete native flora and reduce forage value. In some cases, this plant in excess can be toxic to livestock.

Cenchrus clandestinus

Kikuyu grass  

Status: Invasive

Country/Continent of Origin: E. Africa

How it got here: Introduced to California as a means of erosion control

Effects on the ecosystem: This aggressive weed grows quickly and has been found to smother native species, degrading biodiversity. 

Echium candicans

Pride of Madeira

Status: Invasive

Country/Continent of Origin: Madeira and the Canary Islands 

How it got here: Brought to the U.S. through the horticultural and ornamental trade

Effects on the ecosystem: CAL-IPC (California Invasive Plant Council) states that this plant moves into native habitats slowly and has minor impacts on a statewide basis; however, it can grow more aggressively and cause more damage, such as outcompeting natives and reducing biodiversity in other parts of the state

Malva parviflora

Cheeseweed mallow

Status: Non-native

Country/Continent of Origin:

How it got here:

Effects on the ecosystem: In certain circumstances, this weed can accumulate nitrates, which can be toxic to cattle and grazers. It can also host many plant viruses that are harmful to the surrounding vegetation. It also chokes out other native species, reducing biodiversity.

Plantago Coronopus

Buckhorn Plantain

Status: Non-native or invasive depending on locality

Country/Continent of Origin: Eurasia

How it got here: Not well understood, theorized to have been brought over in grass seed

Effects on the ecosystem: This plant is considered harmful to native plants and livestock and displaces pollinators.
